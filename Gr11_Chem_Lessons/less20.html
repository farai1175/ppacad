Definitions and Scientific Principles

Solution (LO 1):

A homogeneous (uniform) mixture of solute and solvent. A solution forms when a solute dissolves in a solvent.

Solvent (LO 1):

The substance that dissolves a solute to form a solution. The most common solvent is liquid water, which results in an aqueous solution.

Solute (LO 1):

The substance that is dissolved in the solvent.

Concentration (LO 2):

The amount of solute per litre/cubic decimetre (dm³) of solution. Molar concentration (c) is number of moles of solute per volume of solution in litres.

Molar Concentration Formula (LO 2):

C = n/V. Alternatively, c = m/(MV).

Units (LO 2):

Concentration (c): mol·dm⁻³. Volume (V): dm³. Moles (n): mol.

Concentration Principles:

The concentration of a solution determines how "strong" or "weak" the solution is. In titration, a solution of known concentration (the titrant) is used to determine the concentration of an unknown solution (the analyte).

Introduction

Quantitative chemistry requires understanding of precise amounts of substances involved in reactions. When reactions take place in solutions, we must describe the relationship between the amount of dissolved material and the volume of the mixture. This relationship is quantified by concentration. Concentrations are discussed in Module 5, Chemical Change (Part 1). The concentration of reacting solutions is a key topic in stoichiometry.

Molar concentration is a common measurement in chemistry, often represented by symbol c, or by square brackets around the solute formula, e.g., [NaCl]. To work effectively with concentration calculations, learners must first be able to identify the components of a solution and utilize the correct SI units for volume.

Main Body

A solution is defined as a homogeneous mixture formed when one substance dissolves into another. The components are differentiated based on their role:

  • Solvent: This is the substance that does the dissolving. Water is the most common solvent, resulting in an aqueous solution.
  • Solute: This is the substance that gets dissolved in the solvent.

Both solute and solvent can be gases, liquids, or solids.

The volume (V) used in molar concentration calculations must be in cubic decimetres (dm³). This is because molar concentration is defined as moles per cubic decimetre (mol·dm⁻³).

Unit Conversion Factor Example
Litre (L) 1 dm³ 1000 cm³
Millilitre (ml) 0.001 dm³ 1 cm³
Cubic Centimetre (cm³) 0.001 dm³ 1 ml

Conversion Steps (Example):

  1. To convert a volume given in cm³ to dm³, you must divide by 1000.
  2. E.g., 500 cm³ is converted to dm³: V = 500/1000 = 0.5 dm³.

Molar concentration (c) is the amount of solute (n) dissolved in every 1 dm³ of solution.

The relationship is expressed by the formula:

C = n/V

Where:

  • C is concentration, measured in mol·dm⁻³.
  • n is the amount of solute in moles.
  • V is the volume of solution in dm³.

This formula can be rearranged to find the number of moles (n = cV) or the volume (V = n/c).

If mass (m) and molar mass (M) are known instead of moles (n), we use n = m/M to derive an alternative concentration formula:

c = m/(MV)

Steps for Calculation (Based on Worked Examples):

  1. Convert Volume: Ensure the volume (V) is converted to dm³ (if given in cm³ or ml).
  2. Calculate Moles (n): If mass is given, calculate moles using n = m/M.
  3. Substitute: Substitute the moles (n) and volume (V) into the formula C = n/V.
  4. Solve: Calculate the concentration in mol·dm⁻³.

Conclusion

Concentration is a fundamental concept used to describe solutions, which are uniform mixtures of a solute dissolved in a solvent. The molar concentration (c) quantifies this by expressing the amount of solute (in moles, n) per unit volume (in dm³, V), using the equation C = n/V. Absolute consistency in units is vital; thus, volume units must always be converted to dm³ for accurate calculation. This quantitative skill is foundational for advanced concepts like titration, which utilizes standard solutions (solutions of known concentration) to find unknown concentrations.

Understanding concentration is like measuring the strength of your coffee: solute (coffee grounds) dissolves in the solvent (water) to form a solution. Knowing the concentration (molar strength) requires knowing exactly how much coffee (moles) is dissolved in a specific amount of liquid (volume in dm³) so you can consistently replicate the desired strength.
