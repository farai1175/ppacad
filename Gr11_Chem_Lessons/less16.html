Definitions and Scientific Principles

Gay-Lussac's Law:

States that the pressure (p) of a fixed amount of gas is directly proportional to its absolute temperature (T) at constant volume.

Pressure (p):

The pressure exerted by a gas is due to the collision of molecules with the walls of the container.

Absolute Temperature (T):

Temperature measured in kelvin (K), where 0 K represents the absolute zero of temperature.

Standard Temperature and Pressure (STP):

Agreed-upon conditions used by scientists globally to compare results of experiments involving gases.

Kinetic Theory:

The temperature (T) of a gas is a measure of the average kinetic energy of molecules. If temperature increases, the kinetic energy of particles also increases, causing them to move faster.

Direct Proportionality:

When a relationship is directly proportional (p ∝ T), plotting the two variables yields a straight line passing through the origin.

Introduction

Gay-Lussac's Law describes the relationship between pressure and temperature for a gas, provided the volume and amount of gas remain constant. The relationship was noted by scientists around the end of the 17th century and is named after Joseph Gay-Lussac. This law is rooted in the kinetic theory: as molecules speed up (increased temperature), they hit the container walls harder and more often, resulting in an increased pressure, provided the container size is fixed.

Main Body: Gay-Lussac's Law and its Application

Gay-Lussac's law states that the pressure of a fixed amount of gas at constant volume is directly proportional to its absolute temperature.

  • Relationship (LO 1): The relationship between pressure (p) and absolute temperature (T) is directly proportional.
  • Symbols (LO 2): This relationship is expressed in symbols as p ∝ T.
  • Equation (LO 2): Using a proportionality constant (k), the law is written as an equation: p/T = k.
p ∝ T
p/T = k

A Gay-Lussac's law investigation involves heating an enclosed sample of air and measuring the resulting pressure changes.

1. Variables in the Investigation (LO 3):

  • Independent Variable: Temperature (T). This variable is changed by increasing the heat applied, often using a water bath.
  • Dependent Variable: Pressure (P). This variable is measured using a pressure gauge or a meter.
  • Controlled Variables (Constants): The volume (V) and the amount of gas (fixed mass or number of moles, n) must be kept constant. This is typically achieved by using a sealed, rigid container.

2. Taking Readings (LO 5):

In a typical investigation, learners take readings of the temperature (using a thermometer) and the corresponding pressure (reading on a gauge) at regular intervals as the gas is heated.

Gay-Lussac's Law, like Charles' Law, must use the absolute temperature scale (Kelvin) in calculations and graphs.

  • The relationship between the Celsius scale (t) and the Kelvin scale (T) is defined by the formula:
T (K) = t (°C) + 273

The lowest possible theoretical temperature, -273°C, is called absolute zero or 0 K.

1. Pressure vs. Absolute Temperature (P vs T):

Plotting pressure against temperature in Kelvin yields a straight line graph that passes through the origin (0,0). This confirms the direct proportionality.

2. Pressure vs. Celsius Temperature (P vs t):

Plotting pressure against temperature in °C also yields a straight line, but it does not go through the origin. If this line is extrapolated (extended backwards) until it cuts the x-axis (temperature axis), it determines the value of -273°C.

Deviation of Real Gases: At very low temperatures, the forces of attraction between real gas particles become significant, causing them to move slower and exert less pressure than predicted by the law. Consequently, the graph of a real gas deviates from the straight line of an ideal gas at low temperatures.

1. Calculation using the Derived Formula:

Because the ratio of pressure to absolute temperature is constant (p/T = k), the formula used for calculations involving changing conditions is:

p₁/T₁ = p₂/T₂
  • It is critical that temperature values T₁ and T₂ are always in kelvin.
  • Pressure units (p₁ and p₂) must be the same throughout the calculation (e.g., Pa or atm or kPa).

2. Standard Temperature and Pressure (STP) Application:

Scientists agree to report results after converting the gas volume to what it would be at STP. STP values are used in calculations, often involving the combined or ideal gas laws, to compare experimental results globally.

Standard Temperature
273 K (0°C)
Standard Pressure
101.3 kPa (1.013 × 10⁵ Pa)

Conclusion

Gay-Lussac's Law establishes the direct proportionality between the pressure and the absolute temperature of a fixed amount of gas held at constant volume. This law is explained by the kinetic theory: increased temperature increases molecular kinetic energy, leading to more frequent and harder collisions with the container walls, thus increasing pressure. Successfully applying this law, either graphically or mathematically (p₁/T₁ = p₂/T₂), demands the use of the Kelvin temperature scale and knowledge of the agreed-upon conditions for Standard Temperature and Pressure (STP).
