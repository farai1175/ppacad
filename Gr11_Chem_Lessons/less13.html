Definitions and Scientific Principles

Kinetic Molecular Theory:

A model that explains the behaviour of gases using the motion of atoms and molecules.

Elastic Collision:

A collision where the total kinetic energy before and after the collision is constant.

Pressure (p):

The force that acts per unit surface area. Pressure in a gas is a result of gas molecules colliding with the walls of a container.

Temperature (T):

A measure of the average kinetic energy of the molecules of the gas.

Ideal Gas (Perfect Gas):

A theoretical gas which obeys the gas laws under all conditions of temperature and pressure.

Kinetic Theory Assumptions (for gases):

Gas molecules move constantly, attract each other only weakly (except when close enough for repulsion to act), and molecules travel in straight lines except when colliding.

Ideal Gas Properties:

Ideal gas particles occupy zero volume, exert no forces on each other except during a collision, and collide with perfectly elastic collisions.

Introduction

The Gas Laws (Boyle's law, Charles' law, etc.) describe how gases behave mathematically, but they do not explain why. The explanation for the macroscopic behaviour of gases—such as their pressure and temperature—comes from the Kinetic Theory of Gases. This theory is a model that describes the motion of individual molecules, their kinetic energy, and their collisions, enabling us to understand how these properties are interrelated.

Main Body: Principles of the Kinetic Theory

According to the Kinetic Molecular Theory, matter is made up of particles (atoms or molecules) that move constantly.

  • Gas molecules move randomly.
  • They travel in straight lines, except when they collide with each other or the walls of a container.
  • They move at different speeds.

Gas molecules are in constant motion and collide with each other and the walls of the container.

  • These collisions are assumed to be perfectly elastic.
  • An elastic collision means that the total kinetic energy before and after the collisions is constant. However, the kinetic energy of the individual molecules can change during the collision.
  • In ideal gases, molecules only interact when they collide, as intermolecular forces between them are assumed to be non-existent.

In any sample of gas, the atoms or molecules all move at different speeds—some moving slowly, and some moving fast.

  • To make sense of this variety of movements and describe the system as a whole, it is necessary to work with the average speed of the particles.
  • For an ideal gas, it is simplified: the theory assumes the atoms or molecules move at the same speed. For a real gas, however, the particles truly move at different speeds.

The temperature (T) of a gas is a measure of the average kinetic energy (Ek) of the molecules.

  • If the gas is heated, the average kinetic energy of the gas particles will increase.
  • The higher the temperature, the faster the molecules move, and the greater the average kinetic energy.
  • When temperature is measured in Kelvin (K), this number is directly proportional to the average kinetic energy of the particles.
T ∝ Average Ek

Pressure in a gas is a result of the gas molecules colliding with the walls of a container.

  • Pressure is defined as the force that acts per unit surface area (P = F/A).
  • The magnitude of the pressure is determined by the number of collisions on a specific area and the intensity of the collisions.
  • If the temperature increases, gas particles move faster, striking the walls of the container harder and more often, so pressure increases.
  • If the volume decreases, particles travel shorter distances and strike the walls more often, causing pressure to increase.
P = F/A

An ideal gas (or perfect gas) is a theoretical model that assumes the gas obeys the gas laws under all conditions of temperature and pressure.

The properties of an ideal gas based on molecular motion are:

  1. The particles are identical in all ways.
  2. The particles occupy zero volume.
  3. The particles exert no forces on each other except during a collision.
  4. Collisions are perfectly elastic (no energy is lost or gained in total).
  5. In this model, the atoms or molecules move at the same speed.
Note on Real Gases:

The molecules of a real gas usually behave much like an ideal gas under normal conditions, but they deviate at very low temperatures (attractive forces become significant) and very high pressures (molecular volume becomes significant).

The kinetic theory provides the reason behind the mathematical relationships defined by the gas laws:

Boyle's Law (P ∝ 1/V at constant T):

If the volume (V) decreases, the gas particles are confined to a smaller area and travel shorter distances. Since the temperature (average Ek) remains constant, the particles strike the walls of the container more often (more collisions per unit area), thus increasing the pressure (P).

P ∝ 1/V (at constant T)
Charles' Law (V ∝ T at constant P):

If the temperature (T) increases, the average kinetic energy and speed of the particles increase. These faster molecules collide with the container walls more often and with greater impact. To maintain constant pressure, the collisions must be spread over a larger area, forcing the volume (V) to increase.

V ∝ T (at constant P)
Gay-Lussac's Law (P ∝ T at constant V):

If the temperature (T) increases, the average kinetic energy and speed of the gas molecules increase. Since the volume is fixed, the faster molecules strike the container walls harder and more frequently (more intense and frequent collisions), resulting in an increase in pressure (P).

P ∝ T (at constant V)

Conclusion

The Kinetic Theory of Gases is a powerful model that explains gas behaviour by describing the constant, random motion of particles, their elastic collisions, and their resultant average kinetic energy. This kinetic energy directly relates to the measurable temperature of the gas, while the frequency and intensity of molecular collisions with the walls determine the gas pressure. By applying these fundamental molecular principles, we can theoretically validate and explain the empirical relationships defined by the Gas Laws.
