Definitions and Scientific Principles

▼
Term Definition/Principle
Acid-Base Indicator A weak acid, or a weak base, that changes colour as the H⁺ ion concentration or the OH⁻ ion concentration in a solution changes. They are coloured dyes.
Neutralisation Reaction The reaction between an acid and a base, typically producing a salt and water. These reactions are usually exothermic.
Salt The product formed in an acid-base reaction, made up of the cation from the base and the anion from the acid. All salts are ionic substances.

Introduction

▼

Acid-base reactions are categorized as neutralisation reactions, which are proton transfer reactions that often result in the formation of a salt and water. Since reactants and products in these reactions are often clear solutions, we rely on special chemical compounds called indicators to track the reaction progress. Precise quantitative analysis of these solutions, particularly finding unknown concentrations, is performed using a technique known as titration (volumetric analysis).

1. Acid-Base Indicators and Colour Changes

Indicators are essential in chemistry as they visually display the acidity or alkalinity (pH) of a solution. They are typically weak acids or weak bases whose molecular structure changes in response to the concentration of H⁺ or OH⁻ ions present, resulting in a distinct colour change.

2. Neutralisation Reactions

Neutralisation occurs when the properties of an acid and a base cancel each other out, often producing an ionic salt and water. The reaction is generally exothermic, meaning energy is released.

Acid + Metal Hydroxide

→ Salt + Water

HCl(aq) + NaOH(aq) → NaCl(aq) + H₂O(l)

Acid + Metal Oxide

→ Salt + Water

2HCl(aq) + MgO(s) → MgCl₂(aq) + H₂O(l)

Acid + Metal Carbonate

→ Salt + Water + CO₂

2HCl(aq) + Na₂CO₃(aq) → 2NaCl(aq) + H₂O(l) + CO₂(g)

3. Identifying Reactants for Salt Preparation

The composition of a salt is determined by the reactants used. To prepare a specific salt:

  1. Identify the metal cation of the target salt; this determines the base required.
  2. Identify the non-metal anion of the target salt; this determines the acid required.

Salt Preparation Examples

Target Salt: Potassium chloride (KCl)
Required Acid: HCl (provides Cl⁻)
Required Base: KOH (provides K⁺)
HCl(aq) + KOH(aq) → KCl(aq) + H₂O(l)
Target Salt: Sodium nitrate (NaNO₃)
Required Acid: HNO₃ (provides NO₃⁻)
Required Base: NaOH (provides Na⁺)
HNO₃(aq) + NaOH(aq) → NaNO₃(aq) + H₂O(l)

4. Titration and Volumetric Analysis

Titration is a laboratory procedure, also called volumetric analysis, used to accurately measure the volume of a solution of known concentration (standard solution) required to completely react with a fixed volume of an unknown solution.

Conclusion

▼

Acid-base chemistry involves predictable neutralisation reactions that form salts and water. Indicators, which are weak acids or bases sensitive to H⁺ and OH⁻ concentration, allow us to monitor these reactions. Titration (volumetric analysis) is the quantitative method based on the principle of neutralisation, allowing chemists to accurately determine unknown concentrations using a standard solution and the relationship cₐVₐ / cբVբ = nₐ / nբ, effectively linking stoichiometry with solution chemistry.

Key Takeaway: Titration allows precise determination of unknown concentrations using the formula cₐVₐ / cբVբ = nₐ / nբ, where nₐ and nբ come from the balanced chemical equation.
