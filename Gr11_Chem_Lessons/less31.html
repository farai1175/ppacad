Acid-Base Neutralisation and Indicators

Chemical Change (Part 2) - Physical Sciences (Chemistry Focus)

  1. Write equations for acid reactions with metal hydroxides, oxides and carbonates.
  2. Describe acid-base indicators and how they work.
  3. Know indicator colors for litmus, methyl orange, phenolphthalein, and bromothymol blue.
  4. Identify acids and bases needed to prepare specific salts.
  5. Write neutralisation reactions of common acids and bases.

Definitions and Scientific Principles

▼
Term Definition/Principle
Neutralisation Reaction The reaction between an acid and a base. When an acid and a base react, a salt and water are typically produced. During the reaction, energy is released, meaning it is exothermic.
Salt A product formed in an acid-base reaction that results when the metal ion from the base replaces the hydrogen ion in the acid. A salt is made up of the cation from the base and the anion from the acid. All salts are ionic substances.
Acid-Base Indicator A weak acid, or a weak base, which colour changes as the H⁺ ion concentration or the OH⁻ ion concentration in a solution changes. They are coloured dyes that show if a solution is acid or alkaline.

Introduction

▼

Acid-base reactions are fundamental chemical processes, the most common type being neutralisation, where the acidic and basic properties are cancelled out. This process is highly important, used everywhere from industrial processes (like neutralizing acidic SO₂ gas) to biology (like antacids relieving heartburn).

Neutralisation reactions are characterized by the transfer of protons (H⁺ ions) between the acid and base, which results in the formation of an ionic compound, called a salt, and usually water. These reactions are always exothermic, releasing heat to the surroundings.

Main Body

▼

1. Overall Equations for Acid-Base Reactions

Acids react with several types of bases, including metal hydroxides, metal oxides, and metal carbonates/hydrogen carbonates.

With Metal Hydroxides

Acid + Metal Hydroxide → Salt + Water

HCl(aq) + NaOH(aq) → NaCl(aq) + H₂O(l)
H₂SO₄(aq) + 2KOH(aq) → K₂SO₄(aq) + 2H₂O(l)

The essential net reaction is H⁺(aq) + OH⁻(aq) → H₂O(l)

With Metal Oxides

Acid + Metal Oxide → Salt + Water

2HCl(aq) + CaO(aq) → CaCl₂(aq) + H₂O(l)
2HCl(aq) + CuO(aq) → CuCl₂(aq) + H₂O(l)

With Metal Carbonates

Acid + Metal Carbonate → Salt + Water + CO₂

2HCl(aq) + Na₂CO₃(aq) → 2NaCl(aq) + H₂O(l) + CO₂(g)
2HCl(aq) + CaCO₃(aq) → CaCl₂(aq) + H₂O(l) + CO₂(g)

2. Preparing a Given Salt

To prepare a specific salt, you must identify its components. The positive ion (metal cation) of the salt comes from the base, and the negative ion (anion) comes from the acid.

Salt Preparation Examples

Target Salt: Copper(II) sulfate (CuSO₄)
Required Acid: H₂SO₄ (provides SO₄²⁻)
Required Base: CuO (provides Cu²⁺)
H₂SO₄(aq) + CuO(aq) → CuSO₄(aq) + H₂O(l)
Target Salt: Sodium chloride (NaCl)
Required Acid: HCl (provides Cl⁻)
Required Base: NaOH (provides Na⁺)
HCl(aq) + NaOH(aq) → NaCl(aq) + H₂O(l)
Target Salt: Potassium nitrate (KNO₃)
Required Acid: HNO₃ (provides NO₃⁻)
Required Base: KOH (provides K⁺)
HNO₃(aq) + KOH(aq) → KNO₃(aq) + H₂O(l)

3. Acid-Base Indicators

Acid-base indicators are weak acids or weak bases that are coloured dyes. They change color when the concentration of H⁺ or OH⁻ ions in the solution changes, which allows them to indicate whether a solution is acidic or basic. They are crucial in practical work like titration to signal the equivalence point (when the acid and base have reacted completely according to the molar ratio).

Conclusion

▼

Neutralisation reactions follow predictable patterns based on the type of base reacting with the acid, consistently producing salts and often water (and carbon dioxide if a carbonate is involved). The ability to write these equations and identify the required reactants is fundamental chemical knowledge. Furthermore, acid-base indicators, defined as weak acids or bases whose color is sensitive to H⁺ or OH⁻ concentration, provide an essential tool for monitoring and characterizing these chemical changes in the laboratory.

Key Takeaway: Neutralisation reactions always produce a salt and usually water. With carbonates, carbon dioxide is also produced. Indicators help us detect when these reactions are complete.
