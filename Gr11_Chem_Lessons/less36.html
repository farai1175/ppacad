Definitions and Scientific Principles

▼
Term Definition/Principle

Oxidation The loss of electrons. This corresponds to an increase in oxidation number.
Reduction The gain of electrons. This corresponds to a decrease in oxidation number.
Reducing Agent The substance that donates electrons and is itself oxidised. Its oxidation number increases.

Half-reaction Either the oxidation or reduction reaction part of a redox reaction.
Ion-electron method The method used to balance complex redox reactions.

Introduction

▼

In Grade 10, you were introduced to oxidation-reduction, or redox reactions, as processes involving the transfer of electrons between two reactants. Redox reactions are incredibly common, responsible for everything from rusting to the chemical reactions in batteries.

However, tracking electron transfer can be difficult, especially in covalent compounds where electrons are shared unequally rather than completely transferred. To universally track the movement of electrons, chemists use oxidation numbers. Therefore, a redox reaction is defined by two equivalent concepts: the transfer of electrons and the resulting change in oxidation numbers of the elements involved. Crucially, oxidation and reduction always occur simultaneously.

2. Oxidising and Reducing Agents

Every redox reaction involves an oxidising agent and a reducing agent. These terms refer to the reactant that causes the change in the other substance.

Reducing Agent: The substance that loses electrons and causes the reduction of the other substance. Since it loses electrons, the reducing agent is itself oxidised and its oxidation number increases. Metals often act as reducing agents.
Oxidising Agent: The substance that gains electrons and causes the oxidation of the other substance. Since it gains electrons, the oxidising agent is itself reduced and its oxidation number decreases. Non-metals (like O₂) and polyatomic ions (like MnO₄⁻) often act as oxidising agents.

3. Balancing Redox Reactions Using Half-Reactions

Complex redox reactions are typically balanced using the ion-electron method. This method separates the overall reaction into an oxidation half-reaction and a reduction half-reaction.

To utilize this method, chemists often use the Table of Standard Reduction Potentials (Tables 4B), which lists possible reduction half-reactions. The overall balanced redox equation must show the same type and number of atoms, and the same number of charges on both sides.

Steps for Balancing in Acidic Solution:

1 Separate the reaction into two unbalanced half-reactions (oxidation and reduction).
2 Balance all atoms other than H and O.
3 Balance O atoms by adding H₂O molecules to the side needing oxygen.
4 Balance H atoms by adding H⁺ ions to the appropriate side (since it is an acidic medium).
5 Balance the charge for each half-reaction by adding electrons (e⁻) to the side with the greater positive charge.
6 Equalise the number of electrons gained and lost by multiplying one or both half-reactions by a suitable integer.
7 Add the two half-reactions together and cancel out identical species (like electrons or H⁺).
8 Check that the atoms and charges are balanced.

Example: Balancing Cr₂O₇²⁻ → Cr³⁺ in Acidic Solution

Reduction Half-Reaction: Cr₂O₇²⁻ → Cr³⁺
1 Balance Cr atoms: Cr₂O₇²⁻ → 2Cr³⁺
2 Balance O atoms: Cr₂O₇²⁻ → 2Cr³⁺ + 7H₂O
3 Balance H atoms: 14H⁺ + Cr₂O₇²⁻ → 2Cr³⁺ + 7H₂O
4 Balance charge: 6e⁻ + 14H⁺ + Cr₂O₇²⁻ → 2Cr³⁺ + 7H₂O
Final Balanced Half-Reaction: 6e⁻ + 14H⁺ + Cr₂O₇²⁻ → 2Cr³⁺ + 7H₂O

Conclusion

▼

Redox reactions are defined by the simultaneous processes of oxidation (loss of electrons, increase in oxidation number) and reduction (gain of electrons, decrease in oxidation number). The substance that is reduced acts as the oxidising agent, and the substance that is oxidized acts as the reducing agent. For complex reactions, the ion-electron method is used to balance the net ionic equation, often utilizing half-reactions found in the Table of Standard Reduction Potentials (Tables 4B).

Redox Analogy: Financial Transaction

Thinking of a redox reaction is like watching a financial transaction between two people. Oxidation is the seller losing money (electrons) but gaining a higher positive status (increase in oxidation number) relative to their electron count. Reduction is the buyer gaining money (electrons) but decreasing their relative status (decrease in oxidation number). The transaction (electron transfer) must always be perfectly balanced for the reaction to be complete.

Key Takeaway: Remember OIL RIG - Oxidation Is Loss, Reduction Is Gain (of electrons). The reducing agent gets oxidized, and the oxidizing agent gets reduced.
