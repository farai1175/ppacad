Definitions and Scientific Principles

▼
Term Definition/Principle
pH Scale A scale of numbers, typically ranging from 0 to 14, used to express the acidity or alkalinity of a solution.
Acidic Solution A solution with pH < 7. The lower the pH, the stronger the acid. This corresponds to a high concentration of H⁺ ions.
Alkaline/Basic Solution A solution with pH > 7. The higher the pH, the stronger the base.
Neutral Solution A solution with pH = 7.
Auto-ionisation of Water The reaction where water reacts with itself (H₂O) to form hydronium ions (H₃O⁺) and hydroxide ions (OH⁻).
Ion Product of Water (Kw) The equilibrium constant for the ionisation of water, defined as Kw = [H₃O⁺][OH⁻]. At 298 K, Kw = 1 × 10-14.

Introduction

▼

In the previous lessons, we defined acids and bases based on their ability to produce H₃O⁺ ions or OH⁻ ions in water (Arrhenius theory) or to donate or accept protons (Lowry-Brønsted theory). To quantify the strength of these solutions—that is, how acidic or alkaline they are—we use a standardized system called the pH scale. This scale is fundamentally linked to the unique way water molecules interact with each other through a process known as auto-ionisation.

2. Auto-ionisation of Water and the Ion Product (Kw)

Pure water is not just a solvent; it participates in a reversible reaction with itself called auto-ionisation. In this process, one water molecule acts as an acid (proton donor) and another acts as a base (proton acceptor), forming hydronium and hydroxide ions.

2H₂O(l) ⇌ H₃O⁺(aq) + OH⁻(aq)

The equilibrium constant for this process is designated Kw, and it is known as the ion product of water or the ionisation constant of water.

Kw = [H₃O⁺][OH⁻]

At 298 K (which is approximately 25°C), the value of this constant is fixed:

Kw = 1 × 10-14

In neutral water, the concentrations of hydronium and hydroxide ions are equal, meaning:

[H₃O⁺] = [OH⁻] = √(1 × 10-14) = 1 × 10-7 mol·dm-3

3. Calculating pH

The pH of a solution is calculated directly from the concentration of the hydronium ion [H₃O⁺]. It uses a logarithmic scale to handle the typically very small concentrations encountered in aqueous solutions.

pH = -log[H₃O⁺]
1 For strong acids (like HCl or HNO₃), we assume they ionise completely, so the concentration of the acid is equal to the concentration of [H₃O⁺] in solution.
2 For strong bases (like NaOH or KOH), we first find the concentration of [OH⁻] ions, which equals the concentration of the base (assuming complete dissociation). We then use Kw to find [H₃O⁺] before calculating the pH.
3 Use the formula: [H₃O⁺] = Kw / [OH⁻] = 1 × 10-14 / [OH⁻]

Conclusion

▼

The pH scale provides a simple, logarithmic measure of a solution's acidity or alkalinity, ranging from 0 to 14. This scale is dependent on the auto-ionisation of water into H₃O⁺ and OH⁻ ions, quantified by the ion product constant, Kw = 1 × 10-14 at 298 K. The formula pH = -log[H₃O⁺] allows for the precise calculation of pH for strong acid and base solutions.

Key Takeaway: The pH scale is logarithmic, meaning each whole number change represents a 10-fold change in [H₃O⁺] concentration. At 25°C, pure water has pH = 7 because [H₃O⁺] = [OH⁻] = 1 × 10-7 M.
