Introduction

Lewis diagrams help us visualize how atoms share electrons, but they don't show the relative strength of attraction each atom has for those shared electrons. By measuring the tendency of an atom to attract shared electrons, we can determine the degree of polarity in a bond and predict whether the resulting molecule will exhibit a permanent charge difference across its structure. This concept is known as electronegativity, and it helps explain why some molecules are polar while others are non-polar, even when they contain similar types of bonds.

Main Body: Explaining Electronegativity and Polarity

Electronegativity (EN) is a measure of how strongly an atom attracts the shared pair of electrons in a chemical bond. Electronegativity values range from 0.7 (cesium, lowest) to 4.0 (fluorine, highest) and having no units. The higher the electronegativity, the greater the force an atom exerts on electrons forming the chemical bond.

General Trend in the Periodic Table

Electronegativity increases across a period (left to right) and decreases down a group (top to bottom). This is because:

  • Across a period, nuclear charge increases while the shielding effect remains relatively constant
  • Down a group, atomic radius increases, reducing the effective nuclear charge felt by valence electrons

1. Non-Polar Bonds:

A non-polar bond forms when the electronegativity difference (ΔEN) between two bonding atoms is zero.

  • If atoms are identical (e.g., H2, O2, N2), the bond is non-polar.
  • The electron density is shared equally between the two atoms.

2. Polar Bonds:

A polar bond forms when atoms have different electronegativities.

  • The greater the difference in electronegativity, the more polar the bond.

Comparison of Non-Polar and Polar Bonds

The unequal sharing of electrons in a polar bond creates a separation of charge, known as a dipole.

  • The atom with higher electronegativity attracts electrons and gains a partial negative charge (δ-).
  • The atom with lower electronegativity has electrons pulled away slightly and gains a partial positive charge (δ+).

Example: Hydrogen Chloride (H-Cl)

In hydrogen chloride (H-Cl), hydrogen atom (EN=2.1) is partially positive (Hδ+), and chlorine atom (EN=3.0) is partially negative (Clδ-) because the bonding electrons are closer to the chlorine nucleus.

Partial Charges in H-Cl Bond

The bond type can be predicted by calculating the absolute difference (ΔEN) between the electronegativity values of two atoms, always subtracting the smaller value from the larger.

Bond Character Scale

Electronegativity Difference (ΔEN) Type of Bond Polarity of Bond Example
ΔEN = 0 Covalent Non-polar H2
ΔEN < 1 Covalent Weakly polar H-Br (ΔEN=0.7)
1 ≤ ΔEN ≤ 2.1 Covalent Polar (Strong polar) H-F (ΔEN=1.9)
ΔEN > 2.1 Ionic Oppositely charged ions K-Cl (ΔEN=2.2)

The ΔEN value is only a prediction of the most likely bond type, as other factors also play a role. Bonds are characterized as varying from purely covalent (ΔEN=0) to almost purely ionic (ΔEN > 3).

The polarity of individual bonds within a molecule is not the sole factor determining the overall molecular polarity; the shape of the molecule must also be considered.

A molecule may contain polar bonds, but if the molecule has a symmetrical shape, the individual bond dipoles will be balanced and cancel each other out. In this case, the charge distribution remains even, and the molecule is non-polar.

CO2 (Non-polar)
H2O (Polar)

Non-polar Molecule with Polar Bonds (CO2):

  • The C=O bonds are polar (ΔEN=1.0).
  • However, CO2 has a linear shape.
  • The polar bonds are symmetrically arranged around the central carbon atom, causing the dipoles to cancel out, resulting in a non-polar molecule.

Carbon Dioxide (CO2)

Polar Molecule with Polar Bonds (H2O):

  • The O-H bonds are polar (ΔEN=1.4).
  • The water molecule has a bent or angular shape (due to lone pairs on the oxygen atom).
  • Because the structure is asymmetrical, the polar bonds do not cancel. The oxygen side is partially negative, and the hydrogen side is partially positive, making water a polar molecule (a dipole).

Water (H2O)

For a dipole to form in a molecule, the molecule must have polar bond(s) and an asymmetrical (uneven) charge distribution.

Conclusion

Electronegativity quantifies an atom's ability to attract shared electrons, directly determining bond polarity. By calculating ΔEN, we classify bonds as non-polar, polar covalent, or ionic. Crucially, while polar bonds create dipoles, the final determination of whether a molecule is polar rests on molecular shape and symmetry. Symmetrical molecules, regardless of polar bonds, remain non-polar because the bond dipoles cancel each other, whereas asymmetrical molecules exhibit a net dipole moment and are therefore polar.
