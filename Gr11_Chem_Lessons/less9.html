Definitions and Scientific Principles

Definitions
Scientific Principles

Intermolecular Force (IMF)

A weak force of attraction that exists between molecules.

Surface Tension

The resistance that a liquid offers against a force that threatens to increase its surface.

Capillarity (Capillary Action)

The ability of a liquid to flow against gravity in a narrow space, such as a thin tube.

Viscosity

A measure of resistance of a liquid to flow.

Forces of Cohesion

The intermolecular forces within a liquid.

Forces of Adhesion

The intermolecular forces between liquid and solid surrounding surface (e.g., glass tube walls).

IMF and Physical Properties

The type and strength of intermolecular forces are responsible for physical properties of substances, including viscosity, surface tension, and capillarity.

Relationship Between IMF Strength and Physical Properties

As intermolecular forces increase, surface tension, viscosity, and capillarity—generally increase as strength of intermolecular forces increases.

Introduction

While chemical bonds (interatomic forces) hold atoms together within a molecule, the intermolecular forces ($\text{IMF}$s) that act between molecules are responsible for many physical properties of substances. We rely on Lewis diagrams and polarity concepts to predict the type and strength of bonds, but to understand why water has a high surface tension or why honey flows slowly, we must examine the forces that exist between molecules themselves.

Main Body: Explaining Intermolecular Force Phenomena

Surface tension

Surface tension is the result of imbalance of cohesive forces experienced by molecules located in the bulk of a liquid compared to those on the surface.

Mechanism

  1. Molecules located in the bulk of a liquid are attracted equally in all directions by neighbouring molecules.
  2. Molecules located on the surface only have neighbouring molecules on one side, resulting in an inward attractive force.
  3. This inward force causes the surface to contract to the smallest possible area, creating a surface that resists external forces.

Surface Tension Visualization

Surface tension allows insects to walk on water due to the strong cohesive forces between water molecules.

Viscosity

Viscosity is defined as a measure of resistance of a liquid to flow.

Measurement

Viscosity is measured by determining the time it takes for a liquid to flow out of a narrow tube under the force of gravity.

Relationship to IMF Strength

Viscosity depends on the strength of intermolecular forces. The stronger the forces, the greater the viscosity (more resistance to flow).

Viscosity Comparison

Water has low viscosity due to its relatively weak hydrogen bonds, while honey has high viscosity due to stronger intermolecular forces.

Capillarity

Capillarity is the ability of a liquid to flow against gravity in a narrow space, such as a thin tube.

Mechanism

  1. Capillary action occurs when adhesion to the walls of the tube is stronger than the cohesion forces between the liquid molecules themselves.
  2. This creates an upward force that pulls the liquid surface up the narrow tube against gravity.

Capillarity Visualization

Capillary action allows water to climb up a narrow glass tube due to strong adhesion forces between water and glass.

Conclusion

Surface tension, viscosity, and capillarity are physical properties that directly reflect the strength of intermolecular forces in a substance. Stronger forces result in increased surface tension and viscosity, while weaker forces lead to decreased surface tension and increased flow. Understanding these concepts allows us to connect the microscopic forces between molecules to the macroscopic behavior of liquids we observe in everyday life.
