Definitions and Scientific Principles

▼
Term Definition/Principle
Oxidation Number (or Oxidation State) A number assigned to each element in a compound in order to keep track of the movement of electrons during a reaction. It measures the charge of an atom or molecule. It is the charge an atom would have if it was in a compound composed of ions.

Introduction

▼

In previous lessons, we defined oxidation as the loss of electrons and reduction as the gain of electrons. Redox reactions involve the transfer of electrons between two reactants. However, applying the 'loss or gain of electrons' definition (OILRIG) can be difficult in reactions involving highly covalent substances, where electrons are shared unequally rather than completely transferred.

To address this, chemists developed the system of oxidation numbers (or oxidation states). Oxidation numbers provide an expanded definition of oxidation and reduction that works even when there is no clear evidence of electron transfer or ion formation. Oxidation numbers help chemists keep track of electrons in redox reactions, particularly those involving covalent substances.

Main Body

▼

1. Meaning of Oxidation Number

The oxidation number is a formal measure assigned to an element in a compound to represent the charge the atom would have if the compound were entirely composed of ions. It indicates the degree of oxidation (loss of electrons) or reduction (gain of electrons) of an atom in a chemical reaction.

Oxidation numbers are particularly useful for covalent compounds, where electron sharing is unequal due to electronegativity differences, as the calculation treats the more electronegative atom as having captured the shared electrons entirely.

2. Rules for Assigning Oxidation Numbers

To assign oxidation numbers, a strict set of rules or guidelines must be followed. When an element's oxidation number is unknown (like nitrogen, sulfur, or transition metals), you apply the known rules to the known elements and then use the summation rules to solve for the unknown.

1 Free Elements
The oxidation number of atoms in free elements is zero (e.g., Ca, Zn, O₂, Cl₂).
2 Simple Ions
In simple (monatomic) ions, the oxidation number is the same as the charge on the ion (e.g., Mg²⁺ is +2, Br⁻ is -1).
3 Group 1 Metals
The oxidation number of Group 1 metals (K, Na) in a compound is +1.
4 Group 2 Metals
The oxidation number of Group 2 metals (Mg, Ca) in a compound is +2.
5 Hydrogen (H)
The oxidation number of Hydrogen in a compound is usually +1.
Exception
When hydrogen is bonded to a Group 1 metal to form a metal hydride, the oxidation number is -1.
6 Oxygen (O)
The oxidation number of Oxygen in a compound is usually -2.
Exception
In peroxides (e.g., H₂O₂), the oxidation number of oxygen is -1.
7 Fluorine (F)
The oxidation number of Fluorine is always -1.
8 Neutral Compounds
In a neutral compound, the sum of the oxidation numbers of the atoms is zero.
9 Polyatomic Ions
For a polyatomic ion, the sum of the oxidation numbers of all the atoms is equal to the overall charge on the ion.

Conclusion

▼

The concept of the oxidation number provides a rigorous method for tracking electron movement in chemical reactions, especially those involving covalent bonds. It is defined as the charge an atom would possess if the bonds were purely ionic. By applying a standard set of rules concerning the oxidation numbers of common elements like H (+1, except in hydrides) and O (-2, except in peroxides and when bonded to fluorine), we can determine the oxidation state of every atom in neutral molecules (sum to zero) or polyatomic ions (sum to charge of ion). This tool is essential for identifying which substances are oxidized (increase in oxidation number) or reduced (decrease in oxidation number) in redox reactions.

Key Takeaway: Oxidation numbers help us track electron movement in all types of compounds, especially covalent ones where electron transfer isn't obvious. Remember the key exceptions for hydrogen and oxygen!

Free Elements: Oxidation number = 0

Sum Rules

Neutral Compounds: Sum = 0
Polyatomic Ions: Sum = ion charge
