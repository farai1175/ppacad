Definitions and Scientific Principles

Definitions
Scientific Principles

Intermolecular Force (IMF)

A weak force of attraction between molecules.

Vapour Pressure

The pressure exerted by gas molecules of a substance in contact with its liquid or solid form.

Volatil

The ease with which a substance evaporates at a given temperature.

Boiling Point (B.P.)

The temperature at which the vapour pressure of a liquid equals the external atmospheric pressure surrounding the liquid.

Melting Point (M.P.)

The temperature at which a compound changes state from a solid to a liquid, or the temperature at which solid and liquid phases of a substance are at equilibrium.

Covalent Molecular Substances (Small Molecules)

Substances made up of small molecules held together by weak intermolecular forces only.

Covalent Network Substances (Large Molecules/Networks)

Substances made up of a huge network of atoms held together by strong covalent bonds (e.g., diamond, quartz).

Kinetic Energy and Temperature

IMF and Phase Change

The strength of IMF determines the physical state of a substance at a certain temperature and pressure. Weaker IMFs result in a substance being more likely to exist as a gas.

Introduction

Chemical compounds exhibit different physical properties—such as volatility, boiling point, and melting point—depending on the nature and strength of the forces acting between their molecules (IMFs). Understanding these properties requires linking the macroscopic behavior of a substance to the microscopic kinetic energy and attractive forces acting on its constituent molecules. This lesson explores how temperature and IMF strength regulate phase transitions and contrasts the energy required for small molecules versus large molecular networks.

Main Body: Intermolecular Forces and Physical Properties

Evaporation

Evaporation is the phase change from liquid to gas. The ease with which a substance evaporates at a given temperature determines its volatility.

Role of Kinetic Energy and Temperature

  1. Particles in a liquid are constantly moving, possessing kinetic energy. The temperature of a liquid is a measure of the average kinetic energy of these particles.
  2. When heat is transferred to a liquid, the average Ek of particles increases, causing them to move faster.

Relationship between IMF Strength and Vapour Pressure

  • There is an inverse relationship between IMF strength and vapour pressure: The stronger intermolecular forces, the lower the vapour pressure of a substance.
  • Strong IMFs mean that molecules need more energy to escape the surface. Consequently, fewer molecules escape, resulting in lower vapour pressure.

Evaporation and Vapour Pressure

When heat is transferred to a liquid, the average Ek of particles increases, causing them to move faster. Evaporation occurs when individual molecules at the surface have enough energy to break free from the intermolecular forces binding them to the liquid phase and escape into the gas phase.

Example: Petrol vs. Water

Petrol evaporates much easier than water because petrol consists of non-polar molecules with weak London forces, while water has strong hydrogen bonds. The substance with the lowest boiling point, such as ether (b.p. 34.6°C), will exhibit the highest vapour pressure at a given temperature.

For substances composed of small covalent molecules (where IMFs are weak compared to interatomic bonds), melting and boiling require overcoming these intermolecular forces.

Mechanism of Melting and Boiling

  1. As a substance melts, the forces of attraction holding the particles rigidly together in the solid state are weakened, allowing the particles to move slightly apart. The process of melting or boiling involves breaking or weakening of intermolecular forces; the chemical properties (interatomic bonds) remain unchanged.
  2. For example, when liquid water vaporises, the intermolecular forces are overcome, but the water molecules (H2O) remain intact.

Relationship with IMF Strength

  • The strength of the intermolecular force directly determines the melting and boiling points.
  • The stronger the attraction between molecules of a substance, the more energy is needed and the higher the melting and boiling points.
  • Substances with weak IMFs will have low melting and boiling points.

Example: Group 14 Hydrides

The boiling points of Group 14 hydrides increase as molecular mass increases, because London dispersion forces between these non-polar molecules increase in strength with increasing mass.

Boiling Points of Group 14 Hydrides

As molecular mass increases from CH4 to SiH4, the boiling point increases due to stronger London dispersion forces between these non-polar molecules.

The energy required for phase change depends critically on whether the forces being broken are weak intermolecular forces or strong chemical bonds.

Energy Required for Phase Changes

Breaking weak intermolecular forces requires relatively little energy, while breaking strong covalent bonds requires a great deal of energy.

Feature Covalent Molecular Substances (Small Molecules) Covalent Network Substances (Large Molecules/Networks) Source
Structure Small molecules (e.g., H2O, I2, CO2) Huge network of atoms held together by strong covalent bonds (e.g., diamond, quartz, SiO2)
Melting/Boiling Point Low melting points. Small molecules have weak IMFs, so little energy is needed to separate them. Very High melting points. Network solids require breaking strong covalent bonds, requiring significant energy input.

Energy Required Low energy is required to separate molecules. A great deal of energy is required to break the bonds.

Examples

  • P4, S8, and Cl2 have low melting and boiling points because only weak London dispersion forces act between their small molecules.
  • Si has high melting and boiling points because it is arranged in a network structure where covalent bonds link all the atoms in three dimensions, requiring large energy input to break them.

Conclusion

The physical properties of a substance are inextricably linked to the forces between its particles. Evaporation and volatility are inversely related to IMF strength, as determined by the average kinetic energy relative to the forces holding the liquid together. Similarly, for substances composed of small molecules, melting and boiling points are directly proportional to IMF strength, since only these weak forces need to be overcome. In stark contrast, covalent network solids require the breaking of strong interatomic (covalent) bonds for phase change, leading to significantly higher melting points than small molecular substances.
