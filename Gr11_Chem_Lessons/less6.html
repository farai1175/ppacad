Definitions and Scientific Principles

Definitions
Scientific Principles

Intermolecular Force (IMF)

A weak force of attraction that acts between molecules or between atoms of noble gases.

Induced Dipole (Temporary Dipole)

A temporary dipole that forms when the electron arrangement in a non-polar molecule is disturbed by an approaching ion or polar molecule.

Hydrogen Bond

The strongest of the intermolecular forces, which is a special type of dipole-dipole interaction.

Forces and Phase Change

Stronger intermolecular forces mean that more energy is required to overcome these forces for a substance to undergo a phase change (like melting or boiling).

Introduction

Intermolecular forces determine the physical state and properties of substances. The hierarchy of attractive forces ranges from strong ion forces to weak temporary attraction of induced dipole forces. This lesson will categorize and explain the six primary types of intermolecular forces, grouping them to show how they relate to the umbrella term, Van der Waals forces.

Main Body: Naming and Explaining Different Intermolecular Forces

1. Ion-Dipole Forces

This is the attraction between a charged ion (positive or negative) and the oppositely charged end of a polar molecule (dipole).

Ion-Dipole Force Example

When sodium chloride (NaCl) dissolves in water (H2O), the positive sodium ion (Na+) attracts the negative oxygen end of the water molecule, and the negative chloride ion (Cl-) attracts the positive hydrogen end of the water molecule.

2. Ion-Induced Dipole Forces

This is the attraction between an ion and a non-polar molecule. The approaching ion causes a rearrangement or disturbance of the electrons in the non-polar molecule, which momentarily results in a temporary dipole.

Ion-Induced Dipole Force Example

The attraction between ions in sodium chloride (NaCl) and hexane (C6H14), a non-polar molecule.

The term Van der Waals forces refers to a group of weak attractive forces that occur between molecules. This group specifically includes dipole-dipole forces, dipole-induced dipole forces, and induced dipole forces (also known as London or dispersion forces).

Explanation:

These forces occur between non-polar substances (molecules like H2, O2, CH4, or atoms like noble gases). They are the weakest type of IMF. Since electrons are constantly moving, at any instant, they might not be evenly distributed, leading to a temporary or momentary dipole that induces a similar temporary dipole in a neighbouring particle.

Note:

All molecules experience London dispersion forces.

Induced Dipole Forces

Explanation:

These forces occur between polar molecules. The attractive electrostatic force arises when the positive end of one polar molecule aligns with the negative end of an adjacent polar molecule.

Dipole-Dipole Forces

Occurs between molecules of hydrogen chloride (HCl). For molecules of similar mass and size, more polar molecule, stronger dipole force.

Explanation:

This weak attraction occurs when a polar molecule (a permanent dipole) approaches a non-polar molecule or atom and causes a disturbance in the arrangement of the non-polar molecule's electrons, thereby inducing a temporary dipole.

Dipole-Induced Dipole Forces

The interaction between a polar molecule and a neutral atom, where the permanent dipole shifts the electrons in the neutral atom to induce a temporary dipole. This force occurs between oxygen (O2) dissolved in water (H2O).

Hydrogen bonds are recognized as the strongest of the intermolecular forces.

Explanation (LO 3 - Special Case):

Hydrogen bonds are a special type of dipole-dipole force of attraction that is much stronger than an ordinary dipole-dipole interaction.

Conditions for Hydrogen Bonding:

This strong attraction occurs specifically when a hydrogen atom in one molecule is covalently bonded to a small, highly electronegative atom: Oxygen (O), Nitrogen (N), or Fluorine (F).

Mechanism of Strength:

O, N, and F are the smallest and most electronegative atoms in their groups. In a covalent bond with H, they strongly pull the bonding electrons away, giving hydrogen a significant partial positive charge (δ+). The H nucleus is left "unshielded" (it has no other electrons to cover its positive charge) and is strongly attracted to a lone pair of electrons on the adjacent O, N, or F atom of a neighbouring molecule.

Strength Comparison:

Hydrogen bonds have about a tenth of the strength of an average covalent bond.

Hydrogen Bonding

Example: Water molecules (H2O) form hydrogen bonds with each other, resulting in unique properties like high boiling point and surface tension.

Conclusion

Intermolecular forces determine the physical state and properties of substances. The hierarchy of attractive forces ranges from strong ion forces to weak temporary attraction of induced dipole forces. The most important distinction among these weak forces is the presence of Van der Waals forces, which are a collection of three types: dipole-dipole, dipole-induced dipole, and induced dipole forces. The strongest type of IMF is hydrogen bond, which is simply an exceptionally strong version of dipole-dipole force, limited to molecules where hydrogen is bonded directly to F, O, or N.
