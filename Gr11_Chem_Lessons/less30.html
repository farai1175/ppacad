Introduction

▼

Acids and bases are critical chemical compounds, found in everyday life (such as vinegar and baking soda). The reaction between an acid and a base is known as a neutralisation reaction, typically forming a salt and water. To consistently classify these substances, chemists rely on formal definitions, primarily the Arrhenius theory and the broader Brønsted-Lowry theory.

Main Body

▼

1. Defining Acids and Bases

Arrhenius Theory (1884)

The Arrhenius theory was the first formal theory defining acids and bases based on their behavior in water (aqueous solutions).

  • An Arrhenius acid produces H⁺ or H₃O⁺ ions in water.
  • An Arrhenius base produces OH⁻ ions in water.

Limitation: Cannot explain reactions in non-aqueous solvents or how substances like ammonia act as bases without containing OH⁻ ions.

Lowry-Brønsted Theory (1923)

This theory expands the definitions beyond aqueous solutions, focusing on proton transfer.

  • A Lowry-Brønsted acid is a proton (H⁺) donor.
  • A Lowry-Brønsted base is a proton (H⁺) acceptor.

A hydrogen ion (H⁺) is simply a proton. Therefore, a Lowry-Brønsted acid-base reaction is understood as a proton transfer reaction.

2. Conjugate Acid-Base Pairs

In a Lowry-Brønsted reaction, the acid and base react to form new substances that can participate in the reverse reaction. These pairs are related by the transfer of a single proton.

  • When an acid (Acid 1) loses a proton (H⁺), it forms its conjugate base (Base 1).
  • When a base (Base 2) accepts a proton (H⁺), it forms its conjugate acid (Acid 2).

3. Amphiprotic Substances (Ampholytes)

An ampholyte (or amphiprotic substance) is a compound capable of acting as either an acid (proton donor) or a base (proton acceptor), depending on the chemical it is reacting with.

Water (H₂O) as an Ampholyte

Water as a Base

HCl(aq) + H₂O(l) → H₃O⁺(aq) + Cl⁻(aq)

Water accepts a proton (H⁺) from HCl.

Water as an Acid

NH₃(aq) + H₂O(l) → NH₄⁺(aq) + OH⁻(aq)

Water donates a proton (H⁺) to NH₃.

Conclusion

▼

The evolution of acid-base definitions, from Arrhenius's focus on aqueous solutions to the Brønsted-Lowry concept of proton transfer, provides robust models for classifying chemical behaviour. The concept of conjugate acid-base pairs allows us to track proton movement, while the existence of amphiprotic substances highlights that classification depends on the reaction context. Understanding these foundational definitions and the dissolution equations for common acids and bases is essential for predicting and interpreting chemical reactions.

Key Takeaway: Remember that acids donate protons (H⁺) and bases accept protons. Water is amphiprotic - it can act as either an acid or a base depending on the reaction partner.
