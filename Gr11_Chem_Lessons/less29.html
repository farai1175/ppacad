Activation Energy and the Activated Complex

Chemical Change - Physical Sciences (Chemistry Focus)

Learning Objectives

▼

Upon completion of this lesson, students should be able to:

  1. Define activation energy and explain its role in chemical reactions.
  2. Describe the activated complex as a transition state from reactants to products.
  3. Draw or interpret fully labelled sketch graphs (potential energy versus course of reaction graphs) of catalysed and uncatalysed endothermic and exothermic reactions.

Definitions and Scientific Principles

▼
Term Definition/Principle
Activation Energy (EA) The minimum energy required to start a chemical reaction. It is the energy needed to break the bonds in the reactants so that new bonds can form in the products. It is the minimum energy needed for a reaction to take place.
Activated Complex It is an intermediate state in a chemical reaction that is formed while bonds are breaking and new bonds are forming. It is highly unstable and represents the point of maximum potential energy during the reaction pathway.
Catalyst A substance that increases the rate of a chemical reaction without itself undergoing a permanent change. It increases the reaction rate by providing an alternative path of lower activation energy.

Introduction

▼

We learned that chemical reactions involve breaking bonds (which absorbs energy) and forming new bonds (which releases energy). However, simply mixing reactants is often not enough to start a reaction; most reactions require an initial energy input.

This required energy links to the Collision Theory, which states that for a reaction to take place (an effective collision), reacting molecules must collide with the correct orientation and possess enough kinetic energy to overcome repulsive forces and break initial bonds. If particles collide with less energy than the minimum requirement, no reaction occurs. This minimum energy required to initiate the reaction is the Activation Energy.

Main Body

▼

1. Activation Energy (EA)

Activation energy (EA) is the minimum energy required to start a chemical reaction. It is the energy required to break the bonds in the reactants. Since energy must be absorbed to break bonds, the activation energy value is always positive.

2. The Activated Complex

As reactants absorb the activation energy, they reach an intermediate state called the activated complex.

  • The activated complex is a temporary transition state between the reactants and the products.
  • It is formed while the existing reactant bonds are breaking and the new product bonds are forming simultaneously.
  • The system's potential energy is at its maximum when the activated complex forms. Because it is highly unstable, it exists for only a very short period of time before converting either back to the original reactants or proceeding forward to form the stable products.

4. The Effect of a Catalyst

A catalyst is a chemical substance that increases the rate of reaction but remains unchanged at the end of the reaction. Catalysts function by lowering the total activation energy (EA) required for the reaction.

When drawing or interpreting graphs showing the effect of a catalyst:

  1. The catalyst provides an alternative reaction pathway.
  2. This new pathway has a lower peak energy (lower EA) than the uncatalysed pathway.
  3. The effect of the catalyst is shown by drawing a second curve (often dashed or dotted) with a lower maximum peak.
  4. Crucially, the catalyst does not affect the energy of the reactants or products, meaning the heat of reaction (ΔH) remains unchanged.

Activation Energy Calculation

Use the formula to calculate the energy of the activated complex:

Eactivated complex = Ereactants + EA

Conclusion

▼

Activation energy (EA) represents the energy barrier that must be overcome for reactants to transform into products, necessitating an initial minimum energy input. Once this energy is reached, the highly unstable, short-lived activated complex forms. Potential energy diagrams visually map this process, showing the energy required (EA) and the net energy change (ΔH). Catalysts speed up reactions by reducing this energy barrier, lowering EA without altering the final energy difference (ΔH).

Key Takeaway: Remember that EA = Eactivated complex - Ereactants and ΔH = Eproducts - Ereactants. A catalyst lowers EA but does not change ΔH.
