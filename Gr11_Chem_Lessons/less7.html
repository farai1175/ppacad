Introduction

Intermolecular forces (IMFs) are relatively weak forces of attraction that act between molecules or between atoms of noble gases [304, dispersion forces). IMFs are critical because they must be overcome for a substance to change phase. Role of Intermolecular Forces in Density

There is a direct relationship between IMF strength and density: In general, stronger intermolecular forces, the greater the density [307]. More molecules occupying the same unit volume results in a higher density.

Comparison of Phases

The difference in density among the three phases (solid, liquid, gas) is explained by how IMFs hold them together very tightly, maximizing the number of molecules per unit volume. Solids are generally denser than liquids.

In solids, IMFs hold them together very tightly, maximizing the number of molecules per unit volume. In liquids, particles are close but IMFs are very weak, and there are large spaces between the particles. Consequently, there are fewer molecules in a unit volume, resulting in lower density.

Conclusion

Molecular size is a direct determinant of the strength of London dispersion forces: as molecules become larger, their electron clouds also increase in size, resulting in a greater number of molecules per unit volume and, consequently, higher density, which explains why solids are typically denser than liquids, and liquids denser than gases.

End-of-Lesson Questions

1. Recall and Explain (LO 1)

Explain why induced dipole forces (London forces) are stronger between krypton (Kr) atoms than between neon (Ne) atoms, even though both are noble gases. Refer to molecular size and electron clouds in your explanation.

2. Illustrate (LO 1)

Use the phase of halogen molecules (Cl2, Br2, I2) at room temperature to illustrate how increasing molecular mass correlates with increasing IMF strength.

3. Define (LO 2)

Define density and explain how the strength of intermolecular forces affects a substance's density.

4. Compare (LO 2)

Compare the arrangement of molecules in the gaseous phase versus the solid phase, and explain this difference in arrangement using the concept of IMFs and resulting density.

5. Deduce (LO 2)

If Compound X has a lower boiling point and a lower density than Compound Y, what can you deduce about the relative strengths of their intermolecular forces?

6. Exception (LO 2)

Provide a real-world example of a substance whose solid phase is less dense than its liquid phase. Explain the rule this substance breaks regarding density and phase changes.
