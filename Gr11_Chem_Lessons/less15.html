Definitions and Scientific Principles

Charles' Law:

States that the volume (V) of a fixed amount of gas is directly proportional to the absolute temperature (T) of the gas at constant pressure.

Direct Proportionality:

A relationship where one variable increases proportionally as the other increases.

Kelvin Temperature Scale (K):

The temperature scale that begins at absolute zero (0 K). Temperatures measured in kelvin are called absolute temperatures.

Absolute Zero:

The lowest possible theoretical temperature (-273°C) where the molecules of a gas would have no kinetic energy and stop moving.

Constant Conditions:

Charles' Law is valid only when the amount of gas (moles, n) and the pressure (P) are kept constant.

Kinetic Theory and Temperature:

The temperature (T) of a gas is a measure of the average kinetic energy of its molecules. If temperature increases, the average speed of the molecules increases, leading to more frequent and stronger collisions.

Introduction

The behavior of gases is defined by three fundamental relationships: Boyle's law, Charles' law, and Gay-Lussac's law. Charles' Law describes how the volume of a gas changes when its temperature changes, provided the pressure remains fixed. This relationship explains everyday phenomena, such as why balloons shrink in cold water and expand when heated. The key to correctly stating and using Charles' Law is recognizing that temperature must be measured on the absolute (Kelvin) scale.

Main Body: Principles of Charles' Law

Charles' Law established that the volume (V) and the absolute temperature (T) of a fixed amount of gas at constant pressure are directly proportional.

  • This means that if you double the absolute temperature of a gas, the volume also doubles (at constant pressure).
  • Similarly, if you decrease the temperature to one quarter of its value, the volume also decreases to one quarter.
  • In symbols: V ∝ T.

Charles' law states that the volume of a fixed amount of gas is directly proportional to the absolute temperature of the gas at constant pressure.

Mathematically, this proportionality can be converted into an equation: V/T = k, where k is a constant.

V ∝ T
V/T = k

In gas law calculations, temperature must always be measured in kelvin (K).

  • The Celsius temperature scale is problematic because 0°C (the freezing point of water) is not true zero temperature.
  • The absolute zero of temperature is defined as -273°C or 0 K.
  • The formula for converting between Celsius temperature (t) and Kelvin temperature (T) is:
T (K) = t (°C) + 273

An experiment to verify Charles' Law involves measuring the volume of a gas sample at different temperatures while ensuring the amount of gas and the pressure remain constant.

1. Taking Readings and Variables (LO 4):

  • Apparatus often includes a syringe with a stopper placed in a glass beaker filled with water and a thermometer.
  • The apparatus is initially placed in ice water, and then heated slowly.
  • Readings of the temperature (t or T) and the corresponding volume (V) are taken at regular intervals.

The variables are:

  • Independent Variable: Temperature (T or t), which is intentionally changed by heating the water.
  • Dependent Variable: Volume (V), which changes as a result of the temperature change.
  • Controlled Variables (Constants): Pressure and the amount of gas.

2. Plotting Graphs (LO 5):

  • Volume versus Kelvin Temperature (V vs T): Plotting volume against absolute temperature yields a straight line graph that passes through the origin. This straight line confirms that volume is directly proportional to the absolute temperature.
  • Volume versus Celsius Temperature (V vs t): Plotting volume against temperature in °C results in a straight line that does not pass through the origin. By extrapolating (extending) the line backwards until it cuts the x-axis (the temperature axis), the value of -273°C (absolute zero) is found.

Since the ratio of volume to absolute temperature is constant (V/T = k), Charles' Law is written mathematically for calculations involving changing conditions:

V₁/T₁ = V₂/T₂

When performing calculations:

  1. All temperatures given in degrees Celsius must first be converted to Kelvin by adding 273.
  2. The volume units (V₁ and V₂) must be the same, but they do not need to be SI units.

Conclusion

Charles' Law demonstrates the direct proportionality between the volume and the absolute temperature of a gas when pressure and amount are constant. This relationship is verifiable through experimentation, yielding a straight line when volume is plotted against temperature in Kelvin, which passes through the origin. Mastery of Charles' Law relies on the strict adherence to the Kelvin temperature scale in all calculations, as defined by the relationship K = °C + 273.
