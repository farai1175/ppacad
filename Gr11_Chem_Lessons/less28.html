Exothermic and Endothermic Reactions

Chemical Change (Part 2) - Physical Sciences (Chemistry Focus)

Learning Objectives

▼

Upon completion of this lesson, students should be able to:

  1. State that ΔH > 0 for endothermic reactions, i.e., reactions in which energy is absorbed.
  2. State that ΔH < 0 for exothermic reactions, i.e., reactions in which energy is released.

Definitions and Scientific Principles

▼
Term Definition/Principle
Enthalpy Change (ΔH) Also called the heat of reaction. It is the energy absorbed or released per mole in a chemical reaction. It is defined as the heat added to a system under conditions of standard pressure. ΔH is calculated as the difference between the energy of the products (Eprod) and the energy of the reactants (Ereact): ΔH = Eprod - Ereact.
Exothermic Reaction A chemical reaction during which heat energy is transferred to the surroundings. This reaction releases energy in the form of heat or light. For these reactions, the energy of the products is less than the energy of the reactants.
Endothermic Reaction A chemical reaction during which heat energy is taken in from the surroundings. This reaction absorbs energy in the form of heat or light. For these reactions, the energy of the products is greater than the energy of the reactants.

Introduction

▼

Chemical reactions inevitably involve energy changes. When a chemical reaction occurs, energy is always transferred between the reacting system and its surroundings. This energy transfer results from the breaking of existing chemical bonds (which absorbs energy) and the forming of new bonds (which releases energy). The net energy change resulting from these processes determines whether the reaction is classified as exothermic or endothermic. The measurement used to quantify this net energy transfer is the enthalpy change (ΔH).

Main Body: Relating ΔH to Reaction Type

▼

The sign of the enthalpy change (ΔH) is the defining characteristic used to classify a reaction as exothermic or endothermic, indicating the direction of heat flow between the system and the surroundings.

1. Exothermic Reactions: Energy Released (ΔH < 0)

  1. Energy Flow and Definition: An exothermic reaction is characterized by the release of energy into the environment (surroundings). Heat flows from the system to the surroundings.
  2. Enthalpy Sign: For an exothermic reaction, the enthalpy change (ΔH) is negative (ΔH < 0).
  3. Reasoning: The total energy released during the formation of new bonds in the products is greater than the total energy absorbed to break the bonds in the reactants. Consequently, the products have less energy than the reactants.
    • Example representation: A + B → AB + heat or A + B → AB (ΔH < 0).
  4. Observation: Since energy is released to the surroundings, the temperature of the reaction mixture or the surroundings will increase (it feels warmer/hotter).

2. Endothermic Reactions: Energy Absorbed (ΔH > 0)

  1. Energy Flow and Definition: An endothermic reaction is characterized by the absorption of energy from the environment (surroundings). Heat flows from the surroundings to the system.
  2. Enthalpy Sign: For an endothermic reaction, the enthalpy change (ΔH) is positive (ΔH > 0).
  3. Reasoning: The total energy absorbed during the breaking of bonds in the reactants is greater than the total energy released when forming the new bonds in the products. Consequently, the products have more energy than the reactants.
    • Example representation: Reactants + Energy → Products or A + B → AB (ΔH > 0).
  4. Observation: Since energy is absorbed from the surroundings, the temperature of the reaction mixture or the surroundings will decrease (it feels colder/cooler).

3. Summary of ΔH Signs

Reaction Type Energy Flow Energy Comparison (Eprod vs Ereact) Sign of ΔH Condition for ΔH
Exothermic Released to surroundings Eproducts < Ereactants Negative ΔH < 0
Endothermic Absorbed from surroundings Eproducts > Ereactants Positive ΔH > 0

Conclusion

▼

The sign of the enthalpy change (ΔH) immediately classifies a reaction regarding its energy flow. Exothermic reactions release energy into the surroundings, resulting in a negative ΔH (ΔH < 0). Conversely, endothermic reactions absorb energy from the surroundings, resulting in a positive ΔH (ΔH > 0). This fundamental concept is crucial for understanding energy transformation in chemical systems.

Key Takeaway: Remember that ΔH = Eproducts - Ereactants. If products have less energy than reactants, ΔH is negative (exothermic). If products have more energy than reactants, ΔH is positive (endothermic).
