Definitions and Scientific Principles

Definitions
Scientific Principles

Covalent Bond

A form of chemical bond where atoms share electron pairs to attain stable electron configurations, typically occurring between non-metal atoms.

Molecule

A group of two or more atoms covalently bonded and that function as a unit.

Interatomic Force (Intramolecular Force)

The strong electrostatic force that holds atoms together within a molecule due to sharing or transfer of electrons; these are the chemical bonds.

Intermolecular Force (IMF)

A weak force of attraction that acts between molecules or between atoms of noble gases.

Bond Strength

How strongly one atom attracts and is held to another atom; bond strength influences physical and chemical properties.

Forces and Properties

The type and strength of intermolecular forces influences the physical properties of substances.

Energy and Phase Change

Stronger intermolecular forces mean that more energy is required to overcome these forces for a substance to undergo a phase change (like melting or boiling).

Introduction

In previous lessons, the focus was placed on understanding how atoms bond to form molecules (chemical bonds). These chemical bonds result from sharing of valence electrons, leading to the concept of a covalent molecule. Lewis diagrams helped visualise the atoms and the chemical bonds between them. However, to understand why substances behave the way they do on a macroscopic scale—for instance, why water is a liquid at room temperature or why some substances evaporate quickly—we must consider the forces that act between these molecules. This lesson revises the building blocks (covalent molecules) and then contrasts the strong forces within them with the weaker forces between them, explaining how these weaker forces determine physical characteristics.

Main Body: Intermolecular and Interatomic Forces

A covalent bond is a chemical bond formed when atoms, typically non-metals, share their outermost electrons. A molecule is defined as a group of two or more atoms held together by covalent bonds that function as a single unit.

Lewis diagrams are models used to represent the electron configuration of valence electrons in an atom and illustrate the shared electron pairs (covalent bonds) that hold atoms together in a molecule. Covalent bonds, whether single, double, or triple, result in a stable molecule with lower potential energy than the separate atoms.

Covalent Molecule Example: Water (H2O)

It is crucial to distinguish between the strong forces within a molecule and the weak forces between molecules.

Feature Interatomic Forces (Intramolecular Forces) Intermolecular Forces (IMF)
Location Forces that exist between atoms in a molecule. Forces that exist between molecules (or atoms of noble gases).
Identity Known as chemical bonds (covalent, ionic, or metallic). Also known as van der Waals forces (which include dispersion, dipole-dipole, and induced dipole forces), and Hydrogen bonds.
Strength Strong electrostatic forces. Interatomic bonds are much stronger than intermolecular forces. Relatively weak forces of attraction.
Distance Occur over very short distances. Occur over larger distances than bonds.

Description using a Diagram of Small Molecules:

When representing small covalent molecules (like HCl or H2O), the difference is shown clearly:

  • Interatomic forces are represented by solid lines or shared electron pairs linking atoms together to form the molecule itself (e.g., H-Cl bond). Breaking these forces requires high energy.
  • Intermolecular forces are often represented by dotted lines or space indicating the weak attraction between separate, intact molecules. Breaking these forces requires less energy than breaking interatomic forces.

Visualizing Interatomic vs Intermolecular Forces

The strength and type of intermolecular force that exists between molecules have a significant role in determining the physical properties of a chemical substance.

Physical Property Relationship to IMF Strength Explanation/Result
Boiling/Melting Point Increases as IMF strength increases. Stronger IMF means more energy is required to overcome the forces holding particles together to transition from solid to liquid (melting) or liquid to gas (boiling).
Vapour Pressure/Volatility Decreases as IMF strength increases. Substances with weaker IMF (high volatility) evaporate faster because less energy is needed for molecules to escape the liquid phase.
Solubility Follows the rule "like dissolves like". Substances are more likely to be soluble in liquids with similar intermolecular forces. Polar molecules tend to dissolve in polar solvents, and non-polar molecules in non-polar solvents.
Surface Tension Increases as IMF strength increases. Stronger IMF holds surface molecules together more tightly, giving the liquid surface a higher resistance to penetration.

Comparing Physical Properties Based on IMF Strength

Conclusion

Lewis theory explains the formation of covalent molecules, held together by strong interatomic forces (chemical bonds). However, the behavior of these molecules in bulk depends entirely on the weaker forces acting between them, known as intermolecular forces. The strength of these intermolecular forces dictates fundamental physical properties such as melting point, boiling point, and volatility, highlighting their crucial importance in physical chemistry.
