Definitions and Scientific Principles

Definitions
Scientific Principles

Chemical Bond

A chemical bond is the net electrostatic force that two atoms sharing electrons exert on each other. It is a mutual attraction between two atoms resulting from the simultaneous attraction between their nuclei and the outer electrons.

Valence Electrons

The electrons in the outermost energy level of an atom in which there are electrons. These are the electrons that take part in bonding.

Covalent Bond

A form of chemical bond where pairs of electrons are shared between atoms. It is the sharing of electrons between two atoms to form a molecule.

Lewis Diagram (or Electron Dot Diagram)

A model used to predict the arrangement of valence electrons of an atom. In a Lewis diagram, the chemical symbol represents the atom and its core electrons, while dots represent the valence electrons.

Bonding Pair

A pair of electrons that is shared between two atoms in a covalent bond.

Lone Pair (or Non-bonding Electrons)

A pair of electrons in the valence shell of an atom that is not shared with another atom.

Coordinate Covalent Bond (or Dative Covalent Bond)

A covalent bond in which both electrons shared in the bond come from the same atom.

Bond Formation Principle

Atoms will bond if the resulting molecule has a lower potential energy than the sum of the potential energies of the two separate atoms, leading to a more stable compound.

The Octet Rule

Atoms tend to combine in such a way that they each have eight electrons in their outer energy level, giving them the same electron configuration as a noble gas. The octet rule does not apply to hydrogen (H) as it only needs a total of two electrons to fill its first energy level.

Introduction

Lewis theory uses diagrams to visualize the valence electrons involved in chemical bonding. The Lewis diagram model helps predict and explain how atoms achieve stability by interacting with one another. Chemical bonding occurs because atoms seek a lower energy state, typically achieved by completing their outermost energy level, often resulting in eight valence electrons (the octet rule).

Lewis diagrams are based on valence electrons. Electrons that are paired in an orbital are shown as a pair of dots, while electrons that are unpaired are shown as single dots. These single, unpaired electrons are key to understanding the formation of covalent bonds.

Example: Lewis Diagram of Chlorine

Chlorine has 7 valence electrons, shown as dots around the chemical symbol. One electron is unpaired and available for bonding.

Main Body: Rules for Deducing Covalent Bond Formation

The primary method for determining how atoms bond covalently is by inspecting their valence electron configurations and applying specific Lewis rules for sharing electrons.

Statement: Different atoms, each with an unpaired valence electron can share these electrons or form a chemical bond.

  • When two atoms, each having at least one unpaired valence electron, combine, they share these electrons to form a bond.
  • This forms a single covalent bond, where one pair of electrons (one from each atom) is shared.
  • Example: When a hydrogen atom (1 unpaired electron) bonds with a chlorine atom (1 unpaired electron), the unpaired electrons form a single covalent bond to create HCl.

Example: Formation of HCl

Statement: Different atoms with paired valence electrons called lone pairs of electrons, cannot share these four electrons and cannot form a chemical bond.

  • Electrons that are already paired within an atom's valence shell and are not shared with other atoms are called lone pairs.
  • Generally, a paired electron, or lone pair, will normally not be shared to form a bond.
  • Example: In a water molecule (H2O), the oxygen atom has two lone pairs which remain on the oxygen atom and are not shared in the covalent bonds with the hydrogen atoms.
  • Further clarification (No bond formation): This rule explains why noble gases, which already have filled outer energy levels (like Helium, He, with a filled first energy level), do not typically form bonds, as their paired valence electrons cannot be shared to achieve a lower energy molecule.

Example: Water Molecule (H2O)

Oxygen has two lone pairs (shown as paired dots) that are not shared with hydrogen atoms.

Statement: Different atoms, with unpaired valence electrons can share these electrons and form a chemical bond for each electron pair shared (multiple bond formation).

  • If single bonds are insufficient for atoms to achieve a stable noble gas structure (octet) using only their unpaired electrons, they may share more than one pair of electrons.
  • Double Bonds form when two atoms share two electron pairs (four electrons total, two from each atom). Example: Oxygen (O2) requires two bonds to satisfy the octet rule for both atoms.
  • Triple Bonds form when two atoms share three electron pairs (six electrons total, three from each atom). Example: Nitrogen (N2) requires three bonds to satisfy the octet rule.

Examples of Multiple Bonds

N2 (Triple Bond)

Oxygen molecules form a double bond by sharing two pairs of electrons.

Nitrogen molecules form a triple bond by sharing three pairs of electrons.

Statement: Atoms with an incomplete complement of electrons in their valence shell can share a lone pair.

  • A coordinate covalent bond (or dative covalent bond) is a special type of covalent bond where the shared electron pair forming the bond is supplied entirely by one atom.
  • This occurs when an atom or ion that requires two electrons to fill its valence shell (e.g., a hydrogen ion, H+, which has an empty valence orbital) bonds with an atom that possesses an available lone pair.
  • Example: The formation of the ammonium ion (NH4+) occurs when the lone pair on the nitrogen atom in ammonia (NH3) is shared with a hydrogen ion (H+). Similarly, the hydronium ion (H3O+) forms when the oxygen atom in water shares one of its lone pairs with a hydrogen ion. Once formed, a dative covalent bond cannot be distinguished from other covalent bonds.

Example: Formation of Ammonium Ion (NH4+)

The nitrogen atom shares its lone pair with a hydrogen ion (H+) to form a coordinate covalent bond.

Conclusion

Lewis theory provides fundamental rules for understanding how covalent bonds form based on electron sharing to achieve stability (the octet rule). The rules explain how single bonds utilize unpaired electrons, why lone pairs usually remain non-bonding, how multiple bonds increase sharing to meet electron requirements, and the specific mechanism (dative bonding) allowing atoms with empty orbitals to form bonds using a neighbor's lone pair. Mastery of these rules is key to correctly drawing Lewis diagrams and predicting molecular structures.
