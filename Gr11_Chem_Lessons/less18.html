Definitions and Scientific Principles

Ideal Gas (LO 4):

A theoretical gas which obeys gas laws under all conditions of temperature and pressure. The particles of an ideal gas are identical, occupy zero volume, and exert no forces on each other except during a collision.

Real Gas:

Gases that behave more or less like ideal gases, but deviate significantly at high pressures and low temperatures.

Ideal Gas Law:

States that the amount of gas (n) is determined by its pressure (p), volume (V), and temperature (T).

Ideal Gas Equation:

pV = nRT

General Gas Equation:

Used for a fixed amount of gas under changing conditions: p₁V₁/T₁ = p₂V₂/T₂

Molar Calculation (LO 2):

Formula for calculating the number of moles (n) using mass (m) and molar mass (M): n = m/M

Universal Gas Constant (R):

R is constant for all gases and is expressed in SI units as 8.31 J·K⁻¹·mol⁻¹ or 8.31 Pa·m³·K⁻¹·mol⁻¹

SI Units for Ideal Gas Law (LO 1):

Pressure (p): Pascal (Pa); Volume (V): cubic metre (m³); Temperature (T): Kelvin (K); Moles (n): mole (mol)

Temperature Conversion:

Kelvin (K) = Celsius (°C) + 273

Kinetic Theory:

The temperature (T) of a gas is a measure of the average kinetic energy of molecules. Temperature is directly proportional to the average kinetic energy of molecules.

Introduction

In Module 4, we learn that pressure, volume, and temperature of a gas are interrelated, meaning that changing one property affects the others. The behaviour of gases is described by individual gas laws—Boyle's law, Charles' law, and Gay-Lussac's law. When the amount of gas is fixed, these relationships can be combined into a single combined gas law (or general gas equation). This law is essential for relating two sets of conditions (p₁, V₁, T₁ and p₂, V₂, T₂) for a fixed amount of gas.

Main Body

The Universal Gas Constant, R, has a value of 8.31 J·K⁻¹·mol⁻¹. Since a joule (J) is defined as Pa·m³, all variables in the Ideal Gas Equation must be converted to their corresponding SI base units before substitution.

Quantity Common Unit SI Unit Conversion Factor
Temperature (T) Degrees Celsius (°C) Kelvin (K) T (K) = t (°C) + 273
Pressure (p) Kilopascal (kPa) or atmospheres (atm) Pascal (Pa) 1 kPa = 1000 Pa or 1 × 10³ Pa
Volume (V) Cubic decimetre (dm³) or cubic centimetre (cm³) Cubic metre (m³) 1 m³ = 1000 dm³, 1 dm³ = 0.001 m³, 1 m³ = 1,000,000 cm³

Example Conversions:

  • To convert 27°C to Kelvin: T = 27 + 273 = 300 K
  • To convert 100 kPa to Pascal: p = 100 × 10³ Pa
  • To convert 500 cm³ to cubic metres: V = 500 × 10⁻⁶ m³

The variable n in the Ideal Gas Equation represents the number of moles of gas. If the mass (m) or molar mass (M) of the gas is known, the number of moles can be calculated using the formula:

n = m/M

where M is the molar mass measured in g·mol⁻¹. This formula is crucial when the mass of a gas is given instead of the moles, or when the molar mass is needed to identify the gas.

Scientific Notation

An ideal gas (or perfect gas) is a theoretical gas that is modelled to perfectly obey the gas laws under all conditions of temperature and pressure.

The properties ascribed to particles of an ideal gas are:

  • They are identical in all ways.
  • They occupy no volume.
  • They exert no forces on each other except during a collision.
  • Collisions are perfectly elastic, meaning no energy is lost or gained in total.

Real gases—the ordinary gases we encounter—only approximate the behaviour of an ideal gas. A real gas will behave most like an ideal gas under conditions where ideal gas assumptions hold true:

High Temperature (T)

At high temperatures, gas molecules move quickly. The average kinetic energy is high, which allows the molecules to overcome weak attractive forces between them.

Low Pressure (P)

At low pressures, molecules are far apart. This large separation means the intermolecular forces are negligible, and the volume occupied by particles themselves is insignificant compared to the total container volume.

Therefore, real gases approach ideal gas behaviour under high temperature and low pressure. The real gases that behave most like ideal gases under normal conditions are helium (He) and hydrogen (H₂).

Real gases deviate from the ideal gas law under the opposite conditions: low temperature and high pressure.

Low Temperature (T)

As temperature decreases, the average kinetic energy of molecules drops, and they move slower. The attractive forces between molecules (which ideal gas model ignores) become more significant, causing the gas to exert less pressure and occupy a smaller volume than expected by ideal calculations. At very low temperatures, gases eventually liquefy, and the gas laws no longer apply.

High Pressure (P)

When pressure is very high, gas molecules are forced close together. Under these conditions:

  • The volume occupied by particles themselves is no longer negligible compared to the total container volume.
  • Repulsive forces between closely packed molecules increase.
  • The measured volume of a real gas is generally larger than what the ideal gas law predicts at extremely high pressures because the molecules themselves take up space.

Conclusion

The Ideal Gas Law, pV = nRT, provides a powerful mathematical model for describing the state of a gas, particularly when the amount of gas (n) is variable. However, successful application relies on accurate numerical preparation, specifically converting all quantities (pressure, volume, and temperature) into SI units (Pa, m³, and K) and correctly calculating the number of moles (n = m/M). We must remember that this law is a description of an ideal gas, which assumes negligible molecular volume and intermolecular forces. Real gases only conform to this model under practical conditions of low pressure and high temperature.

Understanding the deviation of real gases is like realizing that a blueprint for a perfect house (the ideal gas law) only works perfectly if building materials (gas particles) are infinitely small and never stick together. In reality, materials take up space and attract each other, so the final building (real gas) requires adjustments (high T, low P) to match the original plan.
