Definitions and Scientific Principles

Definitions
Scientific Principles

Valence Electrons

The electrons in the outermost energy level of an atom. These electrons are represented by dots or crosses in Lewis diagrams.

Chemical Bond

The net electrostatic force that two atoms sharing electrons exert on each other.

Covalent Bond

The sharing of electrons between atoms to form a molecule.

Lewis Diagram (or Electron Dot Diagram)

A model used to predict the arrangement of valence electrons of an atom to predict and explain chemical bonding.

Bonding Pair

A pair of electrons that is shared between two atoms in a covalent bond.

Lone Pair

A pair of valence electrons in the outer shell of an atom that is not shared with another atom.

Double Covalent Bond

A bond formed when two atoms share two pairs (four electrons).

Triple Covalent Bond

A bond formed when two atoms share three pairs (six electrons).

The Octet Rule

Atoms usually require eight electrons in their outer energy level to achieve a stable noble gas electron structure (except Hydrogen, which needs two).

Lewis Diagram Components

The chemical symbol represents the atom and its core electrons. Valence electrons are represented by dots arranged around the core.

Introduction

Lewis diagrams are simplified visual models that help us understand how atoms combine to form molecules. They focus specifically on the valence electrons, as these are the electrons involved in bonding. By drawing these diagrams, we can predict the arrangement of bonding and non-bonding electrons to ensure that each atom achieves maximum stability, usually by satisfying the octet rule. The process involves strict steps to account for all valence electrons and distribute them appropriately.

Main Body: Drawing Lewis Diagrams

Drawing a Lewis diagram requires following a structured, multi-step method.

General Guidelines for Drawing Lewis Diagrams of Molecules:

  1. Determine Valence Electrons: Determine the number of valence electrons for each atom (often found from its electron configuration or group number).
  2. Calculate Total Valence Electrons: Sum the valence electrons from all atoms in the molecule to find the total number of electrons that must be placed in the structure.
  3. Determine Structural Arrangement: Draw the structural formula. The central atom is typically the least electronegative element, and Hydrogen (H) atoms are always terminal (bonded to just one atom).
  4. Form Single Bonds: Join the central atom and terminal atoms using single covalent bonds (one pair of shared electrons).
  5. Complete Octets (Terminal Atoms): Place remaining electrons as lone pairs on the terminal atoms until they achieve an octet (or duplet for H).
  6. Complete Octets (Central Atom) and Form Multiple Bonds: If the central atom does not have a complete octet, move lone pairs from the terminal atoms to create double or triple bonds between the central atom and the terminal atom(s) until the octet is satisfied.

Simple molecules primarily involve single covalent bonds, where one pair of electrons is shared. Lewis diagrams for these molecules clearly show the shared bonding pairs and the unshared lone pairs.

Hydrogen Chloride (HCl)
Methane (CH4)
Water (H2O)

Hydrogen Chloride (HCl)

H contributes 1 valence electron, Cl contributes 7. Total = 8. A single bond forms between the unpaired electron on H and the unpaired electron on Cl. Cl requires three lone pairs (6 electrons) to complete its octet, while H achieves a duplet.

Methane (CH4)

Carbon (C) contributes 4 valence electrons, and each H contributes 1. Total = 8. C is the central atom, forming four single bonds with the four H atoms. The carbon octet is satisfied (4 bonding pairs, 8 electrons), and all H atoms have a duplet.

Water (H2O)

Oxygen (O) contributes 6 valence electrons, and H contributes 1 each. Total = 8. O is the central atom, forming two single bonds with the two H atoms. The O atom retains two lone pairs (4 electrons) to complete its octet.

When single bonds do not allow atoms (especially C, N, O, S) to achieve their stable octet, they share more than one pair of electrons, resulting in double or triple bonds.

Oxygen (O2)
Nitrogen (N2)
Carbon Dioxide (CO2)
Hydrogen Cyanide (HCN)
Ethene (C2H4)

Oxygen (O2)

Each oxygen atom needs two electrons to complete its octet (6 valence electrons available). They share two pairs of electrons, forming a double covalent bond (O=O). Each oxygen atom retains two lone pairs.

Nitrogen (N2)

Each nitrogen atom needs three electrons to complete its octet (5 valence electrons available). They share three pairs of electrons, forming a triple covalent bond (N≡N). Each nitrogen atom retains one lone pair.

Carbon Dioxide (CO2)

Carbon is the central atom (4 valence electrons needed). Each oxygen needs 2. C forms two double bonds with the two O atoms (O=C=O). Both the carbon and both oxygen atoms achieve a stable octet.

Hydrogen Cyanide (HCN)

Carbon is the central atom. H forms a single bond with C, and N needs three electrons from C. This results in H-C≡N. The carbon-nitrogen bond is a triple covalent bond, ensuring both C and N achieve octets.

Ethene (C2H4)

This molecule involves a double bond between the two carbon atoms (C=C). Each carbon is also bonded to two hydrogen atoms, satisfying the valence requirement of four bonds for each carbon atom.

Conclusion

Lewis theory provides a systematic approach for representing the valence electrons in atoms and molecules. By strictly following the counting steps and rules, learners can successfully construct Lewis diagrams for both simple molecules involving single bonds and complex molecules involving multiple bonds (double or triple bonds) to satisfy the octet rule. These diagrams are crucial for predicting the shape and polarity of molecules.

1. Recall and Draw (Simple Molecules)

a) Determine the total number of valence electrons for the molecule OF2.

b) Draw the Lewis diagram for OF2. Indicate the number of bonding pairs and lone pairs in the final structure.

2. Application and Deduction (Multiple Bonds)

a) Determine the total number of valence electrons for the molecule CS2.

b) Draw the Lewis diagram for CS2. Explain why single bonds are insufficient for this molecule.

c) Draw the Lewis diagram for the diatomic nitrogen molecule (N2). What type of covalent bond exists between the two atoms?

d) Draw the Lewis diagram for C2H4 (ethene).

3. Terminology

4. Rules Comparison
